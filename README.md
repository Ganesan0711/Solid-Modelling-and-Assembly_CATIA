ABSTRACT
         The Compact Aluminium Linkage Bracket is a lightweight mechanical component designed to transmit motion, force, and moment loads between interconnected members in a reciprocating mechanism. The objective of this project was to design, model, simulate, and validate a structurally efficient linkage bracket capable of operating under combined gravitational and torque loading conditions. The component was developed using Aluminium Alloy 1060 due to its excellent strength-to-weight ratio, corrosion resistance, and manufacturability. A detailed three-dimensional solid model was created incorporating dual pivot bores, a contoured connecting web, and filleted transitions to improve structural performance. Finite Element Analysis (FEA) was performed under realistic operating conditions including a fixed pivot support, gravitational loading, and a remote moment load. The simulation results indicated a maximum deformation of 0.1247 mm and a peak von Mises stress of approximately 39 MPa. The study successfully identified critical stress regions and proposed design improvements to enhance the factor of safety. The project demonstrates a complete product development cycle from concept generation and CAD modeling to structural analysis and prototype preparation.


INTRODUCTION
Linkage mechanisms are fundamental components of many engineering systems where rotary motion must be converted into reciprocating or oscillatory motion. The performance and reliability of such mechanisms depend significantly on the strength and stiffness of the connecting elements that transfer loads between pivots. Among these components, linkage brackets play a crucial role by transmitting forces, moments, and motion while maintaining geometric alignment throughout operation.
Modern mechanical systems demand components that are lightweight, compact, and capable of withstanding complex loading conditions without excessive deformation. To meet these requirements, engineers employ computer-aided design and simulation tools to optimize component geometry before manufacturing.
This project focuses on the design, modeling, and simulation of a compact aluminium linkage bracket intended for small reciprocating mechanisms. The study incorporates material selection, geometric optimization, finite element analysis, and prototype planning to ensure structural reliability and manufacturability. The results provide valuable insights into the behaviour of linkage components under torsional and bending loads and establish a foundation for future design improvements.


PRODUCT DESCRIPTION AND WORKING PRINCIPLE
The Linkage Bracket is a dual-pivot mechanical component consisting of two cylindrical bosses connected through a contoured structural web. The larger bore serves as the fixed pivot interface, while the smaller bore functions as the moving pivot through which external loads and moments are introduced into the component.
The working principle of the linkage bracket is based on force and motion transmission between two pivot points. During operation, one end of the bracket remains constrained while the other experiences rotational movement and torque transfer. As loads are applied, the connecting web transfers stresses between the two pivot regions while maintaining alignment and minimizing deformation.
The component experiences a combination of bending, torsional, and shear stresses generated by the applied remote moment and gravitational loading. To improve load distribution, fillets are incorporated at the boss-to-web transitions, reducing stress concentrations and improving fatigue resistance. The lightweight aluminium construction ensures efficient operation while minimizing inertial effects within the reciprocating mechanism.


CHALLENGES FACED DURING DEVELOPMENT
The development of the linkage bracket involved several design and engineering challenges. One of the primary difficulties was maintaining adequate strength around the pivot bores while minimizing overall mass. Since the component is subjected to torque transfer, stress concentrations naturally develop around the bore regions and geometric transitions.
Another challenge involved optimizing the web profile between the two bosses. Excessive material increased weight, whereas insufficient material resulted in higher stress levels and reduced stiffness. Designing smooth transitions and fillets required multiple iterations to achieve an optimal balance between structural performance and manufacturability.
Simulation setup also presented challenges, particularly in defining realistic boundary conditions that accurately represented the operating mechanism. Proper application of the remote moment load and selection of suitable mesh density were critical to obtaining reliable results. Additionally, interpreting the factor of safety and identifying localized yielding regions required careful evaluation of the simulation outputs to ensure accurate design conclusions.

MATERIAL SELECTION
Aluminium Alloy 1060 was selected for the linkage bracket due to its excellent combination of lightweight characteristics, corrosion resistance, manufacturability, and mechanical performance. The low density of approximately 2700 kg/m³ significantly reduces the overall mass of the mechanism, thereby lowering inertia forces during reciprocating motion.
The material possesses adequate strength and stiffness for moderate load-bearing applications while remaining easy to machine and fabricate. Its excellent corrosion resistance enhances durability in various operating environments. Furthermore, Aluminium 1060 is compatible with both CNC machining and additive manufacturing processes, making it suitable for rapid prototyping as well as production applications.
The selected material therefore satisfies the key design requirements of strength, weight reduction, manufacturability, and economic feasibility.

SOLID MODELLING AND DESIGN METHODOLOGY
The linkage bracket was developed using a feature-based parametric CAD modeling approach. The design process began with the creation of two pivot bosses representing the primary and secondary rotational interfaces. These bosses were connected through a contoured web designed to efficiently transfer loads while minimizing material usage.
Fillets were added at all critical junctions to reduce stress concentration and improve load distribution. Counterbored features were incorporated to accommodate pivot pins, bushings, or bearings. Design iterations focused on optimizing wall thickness, web geometry, and boss dimensions to achieve the desired balance between stiffness and weight.
The final model incorporated smooth geometric transitions and symmetrical features that improved both structural performance and aesthetic appearance. The completed CAD model served as the foundation for meshing, finite element analysis, and prototype preparation.

MESHING METHOD
The finite element model was generated using second-order tetrahedral elements (TE10) to accurately represent the complex geometry of the linkage bracket. Mesh refinement was concentrated around critical regions such as pivot bores, fillet transitions, and load application areas where stress gradients were expected to be highest.

A high-quality mesh consisting of 9,103 nodes and 4,800 elements was generated. Mesh quality parameters including aspect ratio, skewness, stretch, and element angles were evaluated and found to be within acceptable limits. The use of quadratic tetrahedral elements improved solution accuracy by better capturing stress variations around curved surfaces and geometric discontinuities.
The resulting mesh provided a reliable foundation for obtaining accurate stress, strain, and deformation predictions during the finite element analysis.
 
Finite Element Analysis (FEA)
A static non-linear finite element analysis was performed to evaluate the structural behaviour of the linkage bracket under operational loading conditions. The larger pivot bore was constrained using a fixed clamp boundary condition, while the smaller pivot bore was subjected to a remote moment of 1000 N·m. Gravitational loading was also applied to account for self-weight effects.
The simulation revealed a maximum displacement of 0.1247 mm at the loaded end of the bracket, indicating excellent overall stiffness. The maximum von Mises stress reached approximately 39 MPa and was concentrated near the small pivot bore and web transition region. Equivalent plastic strain analysis identified localized yielding in the same region, confirming the presence of a critical stress concentration.
The factor of safety analysis indicated a minimum value of 0.708 near the small pivot bore, suggesting that local reinforcement is necessary for safe operation under the full design load. Overall, the analysis successfully identified both the strengths and limitations of the current design.

PROTOTYPE DEVELOPMENT AND MANUFACTURING
Following simulation validation, the linkage bracket was prepared for physical prototyping. The CAD model was exported in STL and STEP formats for additive and subtractive manufacturing processes. For form and fit verification, FDM 3D printing was selected due to its low cost and rapid turnaround time. The printed prototype enabled verification of bore alignment, assembly clearances, and overall geometry.
For functional testing, CNC machining from Aluminium Alloy 1060 was identified as the preferred manufacturing method. Machining operations included profile milling, drilling, boring, and finishing. The final component was designed to receive a satinated surface finish to enhance appearance and corrosion resistance.
The prototype development phase provided an important opportunity to verify manufacturing feasibility and ensure that the physical component accurately represented the simulated design.

The project successfully demonstrated the complete design and development process of a compact aluminium linkage bracket for a reciprocating mechanism. The CAD model was successfully created, analyzed, and prepared for manufacturing. Finite element analysis confirmed that the component possesses excellent stiffness characteristics with minimal deformation under load.
Although the overall structural performance was satisfactory, the simulation identified a localized yielding region near the small pivot bore where the minimum factor of safety was found to be 0.708. This observation highlights the need for design refinement through increased fillet radii, localized boss reinforcement, or thickness optimization.
Future work may involve topology optimization to reduce weight while maintaining strength, fatigue analysis to evaluate long-term durability under cyclic loading, and dynamic simulation to study the behaviour of the bracket during actual reciprocating motion. Alternative aluminium alloys and advanced manufacturing techniques may also be explored to further improve performance, durability, and production efficiency. These developments will contribute to a more robust and optimized linkage bracket suitable for a wide range of mechanical applications.
